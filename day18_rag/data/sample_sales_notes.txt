SALES TRAINING NOTES — HANDLING OBJECTIONS AND CLOSING DEALS

Section 1: Price Objections

When a prospect says your product is too expensive, the instinct is to offer a discount. Resist that.
Price objections are almost never about the actual price. They are about perceived value.

Your first move is to acknowledge without agreeing. Say something like: "I understand cost is a factor for you. Can I ask what you are comparing us against?"
This opens a dialogue instead of a negotiation.

The most effective technique is to reframe from price to ROI. If your product saves a team 10 hours per week at 500 rupees per hour, that is 5000 rupees saved weekly, or over 2.5 lakh per year. Present that number. The price looks very different next to 2.5 lakh in savings.

Never offer a discount without a business reason attached to it. "I can offer 10% off if you sign before the end of this quarter" is a valid trade. "Here, take 10% off" devalues the product and your position.

Section 2: Discovery Questions

Before you pitch anything, you need to understand the prospect's situation. Most salespeople talk too much in discovery calls. The ratio should be 70% listening, 30% talking.

Good discovery questions follow the SPIN framework: Situation, Problem, Implication, Need.

Situation: "What does your current process for handling invoicing look like?"
Problem: "Where does that process break down most often?"
Implication: "What does that breakdown cost you in terms of time or money?"
Need: "If that was fixed, what would that free you up to do?"

By the time you reach the Need question, the prospect has talked themselves into wanting a solution.

Section 3: Closing Techniques

A trial close is a question that tests readiness without forcing a commitment. "If we could get this set up in two weeks, does that timeline work for your team?"

If they say yes, the close is already done. If they say no, you learn what the real blocker is.

Always summarize agreed points before asking for the decision. "So we have agreed on the scope, the timeline, and the commercial terms. Is there anything else you need from my side to move forward?"

The silence after that question is intentional. Do not fill it. Let the prospect speak first.

Section 4: Handling Competition

When a prospect mentions a competitor, do not attack the competitor. It looks desperate and unprofessional.

Instead, ask what they like about the competitor. Listen carefully. What they like about the competitor is exactly what you need to prove you do better or differently.

Then say: "That makes sense. Here is how we approach that same problem." You are now having a value conversation, not a defensive one.

Section 5: Follow-Up

Most deals are lost because of poor follow-up, not because of the pitch. If a prospect goes silent, they have not said no. They are busy.

A simple follow-up: "Hi, just checking if you had a chance to review the proposal. Happy to jump on a quick call if you have questions." Short, no pressure, specific.

If they go silent for two weeks, try a different angle: share a relevant case study or a piece of news about their industry. Give them a reason to re-engage, not another reminder that you are waiting.

Section 6: Building Pipeline

A healthy pipeline has diversity. You should not have one or two large deals dominating your attention. If they fall through, your quarter collapses.

Aim for three to four times your quota in pipeline value. If you need to close 50 lakhs this quarter, carry 150 to 200 lakhs in qualified pipeline.

Qualify ruthlessly. Use the BANT criteria: Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline. A lead that fails two or more of these is not a real opportunity yet.
